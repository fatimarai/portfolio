Abdominal Ultrasound: What It Is and What to Expect

By Fatima Rai · BMIS, University of Lahore · Published June 19, 2026

If your doctor has recommended an abdominal ultrasound — also called an abdominal sonogram — you probably have questions about what it shows, whether you need to fast, and how long the scan takes. This guide breaks down everything patients should know before, during, and after the exam. You can also review the indications of abdominal ultrasound to understand why doctors order this scan.

What Is an Abdominal Ultrasound?

An abdominal ultrasound uses high-frequency sound waves — not radiation — to create real-time images of the organs inside your belly. A small device called a transducer sends sound waves through your skin, and the echoes that bounce back form a picture on a screen.

It's completely painless, involves no needles or radiation, and is considered one of the safest imaging methods available. A complete abdominal ultrasound typically evaluates the liver, gallbladder, kidneys, pancreas, spleen, aorta, and bladder. For a focused look at liver imaging, read our liver ultrasound guide.

What Can an Abdominal Ultrasound Detect?

Doctors use it to look at:

Liver
Checking for cysts, tumors, or fatty liver disease
Gallbladder
Detecting gallstones or inflammation
Kidneys
Looking for stones, infections, or abnormal growths
Spleen
Checking for enlargement or injury
Pancreas
Evaluating pain or suspected tumors
Aorta
Screening for abdominal aortic aneurysm
Bladder
Assessing size and wall thickness

It's also commonly used during pregnancy to monitor the baby, though that's usually called an obstetric ultrasound.

Why Would You Need One?

Your doctor might order an abdominal ultrasound if you have:

Persistent abdominal pain
Unexplained nausea or vomiting
Swelling in the belly area
Abnormal blood test results
Suspected kidney stones or gallstones
A family history of certain conditions

How to Prepare

Preparation is important because it affects how clearly the images come out. Your doctor will give you specific instructions, but generally:

For Most Abdominal Ultrasounds:

  • Fast for 8–12 hours before your appointment. Food and drink can make it harder to see certain organs clearly, especially the gallbladder.
  • Drink only water and take any regular medications with a small sip of water.
  • Wear loose, comfortable clothing — you may need to lift or remove your shirt.

For Kidney or Bladder Ultrasounds:

  • You may be asked to arrive with a full bladder. This helps provide a "window" for the sound waves to travel through. Drink several glasses of water about an hour before your scan.

Always follow the specific instructions given by your clinic. If you're unsure, call ahead and ask.

What Happens During the Scan

Here's the typical process:

1
Get comfortable
You'll lie down on an exam table. The sonographer will apply a warm, clear gel to your skin to help the transducer make good contact.
2
Scanning begins
The sonographer moves the transducer across your belly. You might feel slight pressure, but it shouldn't hurt.
3
Position changes
You may be asked to hold your breath briefly or change positions (lie on your side) to get better images.
4
Duration
The whole appointment takes 30–45 minutes. The actual scanning is about 20–30 minutes.
5
Results follow-up
The sonographer cannot discuss results with you — a radiologist analyzes the images and sends a report to your doctor.

After the Scan

Resume normal activities
You can eat, drink, and go about your day immediately.
Easy cleanup
The gel might feel cold or sticky — it wipes off easily with a towel or tissue.
Results in 1–3 days
Your doctor will call you or schedule a follow-up to discuss findings.

Is an Abdominal Ultrasound Safe?

Yes. Ultrasound uses no ionizing radiation, making it safe for almost everyone — including pregnant women. There are no known risks or side effects.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does an abdominal ultrasound show?

An abdominal ultrasound shows the liver, gallbladder, kidneys, pancreas, spleen, abdominal aorta, and bladder. It can detect gallstones, kidney stones, cysts, tumors, fluid, enlarged organs, and signs of inflammation or obstruction.

Do you need to fast for an abdominal ultrasound?

Yes, most abdominal ultrasounds require fasting for 8–12 hours. An empty stomach makes it easier to see the gallbladder and upper abdominal organs clearly. You can usually drink water and take medications with a small sip of water.

How long does an abdominal ultrasound take?

The scan itself takes about 20–30 minutes, and the entire appointment usually lasts 30–45 minutes.

Can you drink water before an abdominal ultrasound?

For most abdominal ultrasounds, plain water is allowed and medications can be taken with a small sip. For kidney or bladder ultrasound, you may be asked to drink water beforehand and arrive with a full bladder.

What should I wear to an abdominal ultrasound?

Wear loose, comfortable clothing. You may be asked to lift or remove your shirt and possibly lower your trousers slightly so the sonographer can access your abdomen.

When will I get my abdominal ultrasound results?

Results are usually available within 1–3 days. The sonographer cannot discuss findings during the scan; a radiologist interprets the images and sends a report to your referring doctor.

Key Takeaways

  • Painless, radiation-free, and safe
  • Shows the liver, kidneys, gallbladder, spleen, pancreas, aorta, and bladder
  • Usually requires fasting for 8–12 hours beforehand
  • A full bladder may be needed for kidney or pelvic scans
  • Takes about 20–30 minutes; results in 1–3 days
