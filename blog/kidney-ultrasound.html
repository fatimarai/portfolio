Kidney Ultrasound: What It Shows and How to Prepare

By Fatima Rai · BMIS, University of Lahore · Published June 26, 2026

A kidney ultrasound — also called a renal ultrasound — is a safe, non-invasive imaging test that uses sound waves to create pictures of the kidneys, bladder, and nearby structures. Because it does not use radiation, it is often the first imaging study ordered when doctors suspect problems with the urinary tract or kidneys.

This guide explains what a kidney ultrasound shows, common indications for the exam, how to prepare, and what conditions it can detect.

What Does a Kidney Ultrasound Evaluate?

A renal ultrasound examines the urinary system, including:

Kidneys
Size, shape, position, stones, cysts, masses, and signs of infection or blockage.
Bladder
Wall thickness, bladder volume before and after urination, stones, and masses.
Ureters
The tubes connecting kidneys to the bladder; checked for dilation caused by obstruction.
Prostate (in males)
May be visualized if a transabdominal pelvic approach is used.
Important: This guide is for informational purposes only. It does not replace a clinical assessment. If you have severe flank pain, fever with urinary symptoms, or blood in your urine, seek medical attention immediately.

Common Indications for a Kidney Ultrasound

Doctors order a kidney ultrasound based on symptoms, lab results, or follow-up needs. The table below lists the most common reasons.

Flank or back painKidney stones, hydronephrosis, or infection
Blood in urine (hematuria)Stones, masses, cysts, or infection
Recurrent urinary tract infectionsStructural abnormalities, reflux, or residual urine
Difficulty urinating or weak streamBladder wall thickening, prostate enlargement, or obstruction
High blood pressure (especially young patients)Renal artery stenosis or kidney size differences
Abnormal kidney function testsChronic kidney changes, obstruction, or size abnormalities
Known kidney stonesStone size, location, and whether blockage is present
Pelvic or lower abdominal painBladder stones, masses, or ureteric dilation
Swelling or fluid retentionKidney disease or reduced kidney function
Follow-up after kidney surgery or stentHealing, stent position, and continued drainage
Congenital kidney abnormalitiesStructural problems present from birth

What a Kidney Ultrasound Can Detect

Depending on the indication, a renal ultrasound can help identify:

  • Kidney stones — visible as bright, echogenic structures that cast a shadow.
  • Hydronephrosis — swelling of the kidney due to urine buildup from blockage.
  • Simple kidney cysts — common, fluid-filled sacs that are usually harmless.
  • Complex cysts or masses — may require further imaging or biopsy.
  • Kidney infections — such as pyelonephritis, which can change kidney echotexture.
  • Renal artery stenosis — narrowed kidney arteries, often evaluated with Doppler ultrasound.
  • Bladder problems — stones, tumors, wall thickening, or incomplete emptying.
  • Prostate enlargement — when the bladder and prostate are scanned together.

How to Prepare for a Kidney Ultrasound

Preparation is simple but important for image quality:

  • Drink water before the exam. A full bladder improves visualization of the kidneys, ureters, and bladder. Your clinic may ask you to drink 2–3 glasses of water about an hour before the scan.
  • Do not empty your bladder until the sonographer tells you to, unless you are specifically instructed otherwise.
  • Wear loose, comfortable clothing. You may need to expose your lower abdomen and back.
  • Follow clinic-specific instructions. Some clinics may also request fasting if the upper abdomen is being scanned at the same time.

What Happens During the Scan

A kidney ultrasound is quick and painless:

  1. You will lie on an exam table, usually on your back or side.
  2. The sonographer applies warm gel to your skin over the kidney area and lower abdomen.
  3. A transducer is moved across your skin to capture images of the kidneys and bladder.
  4. You may be asked to hold your breath briefly for clearer images.
  5. The scan usually takes 20–30 minutes.

When Doctors Choose CT or MRI Instead

Renal ultrasound is an excellent first-line test, but it has limits. A doctor may order a CT scan or MRI when:

  • A very small kidney stone is suspected but not seen on ultrasound.
  • Detailed characterization of a complex cyst or mass is needed.
  • Cancer staging or surgical planning is required.
  • Renal artery stenosis needs precise anatomic mapping.
  • Significant obesity or bowel gas limits ultrasound visibility.

Limitations of Kidney Ultrasound

While useful, kidney ultrasound cannot do everything:

  • Small stones may be missed, especially in the ureters.
  • Obesity and bowel gas can reduce image quality.
  • Functional assessment is limited compared to nuclear medicine scans.
  • Deep structures may be partially obscured.

When to Seek Urgent Care

Some symptoms require immediate medical attention rather than waiting for a routine ultrasound. Seek emergency care if you have:

  • Severe flank pain with fever and chills
  • Inability to urinate with lower abdominal pain
  • Visible blood clots in urine
  • Severe back pain after trauma
  • Signs of sepsis such as confusion, rapid heartbeat, or low blood pressure

Frequently Asked Questions

What does a kidney ultrasound show?

A kidney ultrasound shows the size, shape, and position of the kidneys, the bladder, the ureters, and sometimes the prostate. It can detect kidney stones, hydronephrosis, cysts, masses, infections, and structural abnormalities.

Do you need a full bladder for a kidney ultrasound?

Yes. A full bladder acts as an acoustic window, pushing bowel gas out of the way and improving visualization of the kidneys, ureters, and bladder. Your clinic will usually ask you to drink water before the exam and avoid urinating.

How do I prepare for a kidney ultrasound?

Drink several glasses of water about an hour before the exam and try not to empty your bladder. Wear loose, comfortable clothing. Some clinics may also ask you to fast if the upper abdomen is being scanned at the same time.

Can a kidney ultrasound detect kidney stones?

Yes, kidney ultrasound can detect many kidney stones, especially when they are large or cause blockage. However, very small stones or those in certain locations may be missed, and CT is the most sensitive test for stone detection.

How long does a kidney ultrasound take?

A kidney ultrasound usually takes 20–30 minutes. If the bladder, prostate, or blood flow is also being evaluated, the exam may take a few minutes longer.

Is a kidney ultrasound safe?

Yes. Kidney ultrasound uses sound waves, not ionizing radiation. It is safe for adults, children, and pregnant women, and can be repeated as needed.

Key Takeaways

  • A kidney ultrasound evaluates the kidneys, bladder, ureters, and sometimes the prostate.
  • Common indications include flank pain, blood in urine, recurrent UTIs, and abnormal kidney function tests.
  • It can detect kidney stones, hydronephrosis, cysts, masses, infections, and bladder problems.
  • A full bladder is usually required for the best image quality.
  • The exam is painless, radiation-free, and typically takes 20–30 minutes.
  • CT or MRI may be needed when ultrasound results are inconclusive or more detail is required.
