Ultrasound Symptom Checker: A Body-by-Body Guide

By Fatima Rai · BMIS, University of Lahore · Published June 22, 2026

Ultrasound is one of the most versatile and widely used imaging tools in medicine. Because it uses sound waves instead of radiation, it is safe, non-invasive, and often the first choice for evaluating many symptoms.

But with so many different types of ultrasound — abdominal, pelvic, vascular, obstetric, thyroid, musculoskeletal — it can be hard to know which one your symptoms point toward. This guide maps common symptoms to the ultrasound exams typically used to evaluate them. Use it as an educational reference before speaking with your healthcare provider.

Important: This guide is for informational purposes only. It does not replace a clinical assessment. If you have severe, sudden, or worsening symptoms, seek medical attention immediately.

How to Use This Guide

Find the body area that matches your concern, then look for your symptom. The table shows the ultrasound exam commonly ordered for that presentation and what it evaluates. Your doctor may choose a different or additional test based on your full history and physical exam.

Abdomen

Abdominal ultrasound evaluates organs such as the liver, gallbladder, kidneys, pancreas, spleen, and major blood vessels.

Persistent nausea or vomitingAbdominal UltrasoundGallbladder, pancreas, digestive organs
Abdominal bloating or distensionAbdominal UltrasoundFluid, organ enlargement, or masses

Suspected appendicitisAbdominal Ultrasound ± CTAppendix; CT if ultrasound is inconclusive

When to Seek Urgent Care

Some symptoms need immediate medical attention rather than a routine ultrasound appointment. Seek emergency care if you have:

  • Sudden severe abdominal or pelvic pain
  • Heavy vaginal bleeding during pregnancy
  • Sudden chest pain or difficulty breathing
  • Sudden severe testicular pain or swelling
  • One leg that becomes swollen, painful, and warm
  • Sudden weakness, numbness, or difficulty speaking

Key Takeaways

  • Ultrasound is a safe, radiation-free first-line imaging choice for many symptoms.
  • The right ultrasound depends on body area and symptom presentation.
  • Your healthcare provider may combine ultrasound with other tests for a complete diagnosis.
  • This guide is educational — always follow your clinician's specific recommendations.
