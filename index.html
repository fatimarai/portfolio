Comparison of Ovarian Volume in Fertile and Infertile Married Females

A transabdominal ultrasound-based comparative study evaluating ovarian volume differences between fertile and infertile married females.

BMIS

Study

This research case study focused on comparing ovarian volume between fertile and infertile married females using transabdominal ultrasound imaging. The objective was to assess whether ovarian volume measurements could serve as a useful sonographic marker in evaluating female infertility.

The study involved careful patient preparation, optimal transducer selection, and standardized measurement techniques for ovarian length, width, and depth — applying the ellipsoid formula to calculate ovarian volume.

Patient Guide

Abdominal Ultrasound: What It Is and What to Expect

By Fatima Rai · BMIS, University of Lahore

If your doctor has recommended an abdominal ultrasound, you probably have questions. Let's break it down so you know exactly what's coming.

What Is an Abdominal Ultrasound?

An abdominal ultrasound uses sound waves — not radiation — to create images of the organs inside your belly. A small device called a transducer sends sound waves through your skin, and the echoes that bounce back form a picture on a screen.

It's completely painless, involves no needles or radiation, and is considered one of the safest imaging methods available.

What Can an Abdominal Ultrasound Detect?

Doctors use it to look at:

  • Liver — checking for cysts, tumors, or fatty liver disease
  • Gallbladder — detecting gallstones or inflammation
  • Kidneys — looking for stones, infections, or abnormal growths
  • Spleen — checking for enlargement or injury
  • Pancreas — evaluating pain or suspected tumors
  • Aorta — screening for an abdominal aortic aneurysm (a dangerous bulging of the main artery)
  • Bladder — assessing its size and wall thickness

It's also commonly used during pregnancy to monitor the baby, though that's usually called an obstetric ultrasound.

Why Would You Need One?

Your doctor might order an abdominal ultrasound if you have:

  • Persistent abdominal pain
  • Unexplained nausea or vomiting
  • Swelling in the belly area
  • Abnormal blood test results
  • Suspected kidney stones or gallstones
  • A family history of certain conditions

How to Prepare

Preparation is important because it affects how clearly the images come out. Your doctor will give you specific instructions, but generally:

For Most Abdominal Ultrasounds:

  • Fast for 8–12 hours before your appointment. Food and drink can make it harder to see certain organs clearly, especially the gallbladder.
  • Drink only water and take any regular medications with a small sip of water.
  • Wear loose, comfortable clothing — you may need to lift or remove your shirt.

For Kidney or Bladder Ultrasounds:

  • You may be asked to arrive with a full bladder. This helps provide a "window" for the sound waves to travel through. Drink several glasses of water about an hour before your scan.

Always follow the specific instructions given by your clinic. If you're unsure, call ahead and ask.

What Happens During the Scan

Here's the typical process:

  1. You'll lie down on an exam table. The sonographer (a trained ultrasound technician) will apply a warm, clear gel to your skin — this helps the transducer make good contact.
  2. The sonographer will move the transducer across different parts of your belly. You might feel slight pressure as they angle the device, but it shouldn't hurt.
  3. You may be asked to hold your breath briefly or change positions (lie on your side, for example) to get better images.
  4. The whole appointment usually takes 30–45 minutes. The actual scanning is about 20–30 minutes.
  5. The sonographer cannot discuss results with you — a radiologist will analyze the images and send a report to your doctor.

After the Scan

  • You can eat, drink, and resume normal activities immediately.
  • The gel might feel a little cold or sticky — it wipes off easily with a towel or tissue.
  • Results are typically ready within 1–3 days. Your doctor will call you or schedule a follow-up to discuss findings.

Is It Safe?

Yes. Ultrasound uses no ionizing radiation, making it safe for almost everyone — including pregnant women. There are no known risks or side effects.

Key Takeaways

  • Painless, radiation-free, and safe
  • Helps doctors check liver, kidneys, gallbladder, spleen, pancreas, and bladder
  • Usually requires fasting beforehand
  • Full bladder may be needed for kidney or pelvic scans
  • Results in 1–3 days through your referring doctor
